Java Consultant — Keeping Critical Systems Alive and under Control

Laurent Kloeble

WHAT

Systems where a rewrite would be irresponsible — and not refactoring is just as dangerous.

Controlled evolution of critical Java monoliths. Production-first. Behavior-preserving. No big-bang rewrites.

WHAT I ACTUALLY DO

I help organizations regain control over business-critical Java systems that have become fragile, slow, or hard to evolve.

Typical outcomes include:

  • Stabilizing systems that crash, stall, or violate SLAs
  • Diagnosing memory leaks, GC pressure, and performance regressions
  • Making large codebases understandable again
  • Refactoring incrementally, without breaking production
  • Reducing operational risk while enabling future change
This is not about chasing trends.

It’s about keeping systems alive, reliable, and evolvable.

HOW I WORK

I work with teams, not around them.

My approach is shaped by experience as a developer, technical lead, and engineering manager — and by long exposure to real production systems, under real business constraints.

Key principles:

Business outcomes first
Technology is a means, not an end.
Success is measured by user experience, reliability, and business impact — not by framework versions.

Pragmatic by design
I work within real-world constraints: limited time, limited budgets, existing teams, existing systems.
The goal is not to reach an ideal architecture, but to make meaningful improvements that actually stick.

No slideware
My work results in code, analyses, tests, and measurable changes in production — not in slide decks or theoretical diagrams.

Production first
Every change is evaluated against real behavior, real load, and real constraints.

Understand before changing
Systems exist to serve a business purpose.
I start by understanding _why_ the system behaves as it does, before judging _how_ it is built.

Behavior-preserving refactoring
Improvements should not alter what the system does — only how safely, clearly, and predictably it does it.

Measure everything
Performance, memory, latency, error rates — before and after.

Knowledge transfer is part of the job
The goal is not to become indispensable, but to leave teams stronger, more autonomous, and more confident in their system.

I integrate as a peer, contributing to both technical clarity and a shared understanding of the system’s business role.
